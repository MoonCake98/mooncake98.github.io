SCIDS biomarkers used in diagnoses

Biomarkers allow us to diagnose and monitor SCIDS progression.

A biomarker in the context of biomedical research is a measurable indicator of a biological state or process. For example a white blood cell count, a heartbeat or the amount of iron within certain volumes of blood of a patient.

The current way biomarkers are used for SCIDS is for the purposes of newborn SCID screenings, in this manner we specifically target TRECs. TRECs (T cell receptor excision circles) are small circular DNA fragments that are produced as a byproduct during the rearrangement of T-cell receptors and as such the presence of TRECs can be directly related to t cell count which is often a symptom of SCIDS. However as always this is simply an indicator for further investigation.

And if the TREC amount is found to be too differing from the norm the tests will continue to confirm for SCIDS by the use of a functional assay to assess the functionality of the T cell, consisting of the measurement of the proliferation of t cells, activation marker expression, cytokine production and antigen specific immune response.

Currently this is the most accurate and cost efficient method we have for screening for SCIDS, however as DNA sequencing becomes less costly in both time and resources new tests are being developed for the confirmation of genetic causes for SCIDS.
