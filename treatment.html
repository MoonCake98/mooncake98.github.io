SCID

Treatment of SCID

There are several treament options for SCID like stemcell therapy, Gene Therapy and Enzyme Replacement Therapy. Subtypes of SCID can require different treament plans, this will be explained further below.

Overview: The treatment options:

  • Stemcell transplantation (HSCT)
  • Enzyme Replacement Therapy
  • Gene Therapy

Stemcell transplantation (HSCT)

Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) can be used to treat SCID but since SCID does not have the same root cause in all patients, so it's a "heterogeneous disease". This means that the succes rate of replacing The patients stem cells will not always have the same outcome.

In 1968, the first transplant of allogeneic bone marrow was given. And for the first decade after, an HLA identical donor was required to avoid the lethal graft vs host disease. After that, T cell depleted transplants have been possible from HLA haploidentical related donors since 1981. and even more recent from match unrelated donors. 4
Bonemarrow transplantation has resulted in lifesaving T-cell reconstitution in infants with SCID but the correction of B-cell function has been more problematic.

Enzyme Replacement Therapy

In some cases, SCID is caused by adenosine deaminase deficiency. (2) Adenosine deaminase is an enzyme which catalyzes the irreversible deamination of adenosine and deoxyadenosine to insosine and deoxyinosine. This enzyme occures everywhere in the body.

Gene Therapy

ADA-SCID is the first disease treated by gene-therapy. SCID involves multiple genes which leads to makes diagnosis of the disease difficult. In early years many infants were diagnosed almost after half a year and in severe conditions which led to the decrease in the survival rate of the children. But now due to advanced newborn screening modalities and other monitoring systems it can be diagnosed as early as within three months of age.
(5) Gene therapy is very positively effective in treating SCID patients. This process involves introducing a viral vector containing the corrected gene into the patient's own HSCs, incorporating it into their genetic makeup, and then reintroducing these treated cells into the body. It has effectively cured individuals with X-linked and ADA-deficient SCID. However, initial trials faced challenges such as graft failure, and in some cases, the insertion of the vector caused mutations, leading to lymphoproliferation and leukemia, especially in X-linked SCID cases. To reduce these risks, changes have been made to the design of retroviral vectors. This includes using self-inactivating gamma-retroviral vectors with modified U3 regions, as well as lenti-viral vectors. These adjustments aim to minimize the likelihood of mutations during the integration process.>
